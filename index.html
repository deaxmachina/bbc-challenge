On November 7, Indian capital, Delhi woke up to a blanket of thick grey smog. The toxic haze was so severe that politicians announced plans to close schools. Methodology

A report by Berkeley Earth showed that in China, 1.6 million people die every year from an average exposure of 52 Î¼g/m3 of PM2.5. To kill 1.6 million people would require, assuming 1.36 x9^6 deaths per cigarette, 1.1 trillion cigarettes. Since the population of China is 1.35 billion, that comes to 764 cigarettes every year per person, or about 2.4 cigarettes per day.

Put another way, one cigarette is equivalent to an air pollution of 22 Î¼g/m3 (Absolute value: 21.6 Î¼g/m3) for one day.

The average PM2.5 in Beijing over the year is about 85 Î¼g/m3, equivalent to about 4 cigarettes per day. Similarly, if the air pollution in New Delhi, India, is 546 Î¼g/m3, it is equivalent to about 25 cigarettes each day.

We took the PM 2.5 data of 33 cities in India every day for one week. We calculated the mean of 6 days PM 2.5 data of each city and divided it by 21.6 Î¼g/m3 to get the number of cigarettes. It is also imperative to say the numbers shown is the assumption that you ideally spent most of your time breathing air from outside.
